Module 02

Our Goal
& Vibe

Good photos aren't just technically correct — they feel like something. This module is about what that feeling is at HTBB, and how to capture it.

The goal

Our photography should make someone feel like they belong before they even walk through the door. When someone scrolls past an HTBB photo on Instagram, we want them to feel warmth, life, and community — not a polished production.

The goal is never just a beautiful image. The goal is a true image. One that reflects who HTBB really is.

The vibe

Think: warm, natural, candid. We're not shooting a fashion campaign or a corporate event. We're documenting real community — real faces, real moments, real connection.

Shoot for this
Candid laughter during connect time. A hand raised in worship. Two people praying together. Someone hearing the Gospel for the first time. These are the shots that matter.
Avoid this
Stiff, posed, or overly composed shots that feel like stock photography. We want life — not a brochure.
Shoot for this
A mix of wide establishing shots, mid-range community moments, and intimate close-ups — all three make a strong gallery.
Avoid this
Only shooting the stage. The congregation is the story — make sure they're well represented in your gallery.
What this looks like in practice
Wide establishing shot — the full room e.g. congregation during worship viewed from the back
The standard we're after
Photos that look like they belong in a magazine — but feel like they were taken by a friend. That tension between quality and warmth is exactly the HTBB vibe.

People are always the subject

Even when you're photographing the room, the lights, or the stage — the photo only matters if it conveys a human experience. Ask yourself: does this photo make someone feel something?

Aim to capture emotion over aesthetics. A blurry photo of someone genuinely moved in worship is infinitely more powerful than a sharp, technically perfect shot of an empty stage.

Content we need every Sunday

Every week, the communications team needs a consistent set of content types. Think of this as your informal shot list:

  • Wide shots — full room during worship, altar call, or key moments
  • Stage shots — the preacher, the worship team in action
  • Congregation mid-shots — rows of worshippers, hands raised, eyes closed
  • Connection moments — people talking, laughing, hugging, praying together
  • Details — Bibles open, hands, coffee cups, signage (when relevant)
  • Candid portraits — genuine smiles, close-up expressions of joy or reflection
